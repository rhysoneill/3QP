# Expected State Transitions

## Overview

This document defines qualitative patterns of state change that occur within mission scenarios. State transitions describe how crew functioning evolves across physiological, cognitive, emotional, social, and operational domains as events unfold and conditions change.

These transitions are described using purely conceptual language that characterizes the direction, nature, and trajectory of change without numeric specification. The transitions reflect common patterns observed in isolated, confined, and demanding environments but remain flexible enough to describe varied mission arcs.

---

## Physiological Transitions

Physiological state transitions describe changes in sleep quality, fatigue accumulation, stress response activation, and physical capacity.

### Stable Functioning → Emerging Strain

**Trigger Context**: Introduction of stressors such as workload increase, sleep disruption, environmental changes, or sustained elevated demands.

**Transition Description**: 
The crew transitions from a state of physiological equilibrium characterized by regular sleep cycles, adequate energy levels, and stable stress hormone patterns to a state where early signs of strain appear. Sleep may become slightly less restorative. Fatigue begins to accumulate rather than fully clearing with rest. Stress response systems activate more readily. Physical recovery between work periods feels less complete.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Sleep feels lighter or less refreshing
- Morning alertness is diminished
- Physical tasks require slightly more effort
- Recovery from exertion takes longer
- Subtle increase in muscle tension or physical discomfort

**Reversibility**: Readily reversible if stressors are removed and adequate rest is obtained.

---

### Emerging Strain → Degraded Resilience

**Trigger Context**: Persistence or intensification of stressors without adequate recovery. Compounding demands. Insufficient sleep over multiple cycles.

**Transition Description**:
Emerging strain deepens into more significant physiological degradation. Sleep disturbance becomes more pronounced—difficulty falling asleep, frequent awakenings, early morning waking. Fatigue accumulates progressively. Stress response remains chronically elevated. Physical capacity diminishes. The body's ability to maintain homeostatic balance weakens. Reserve capacity for handling additional demands shrinks.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Sleep is fragmented and unrefreshing
- Persistent fatigue regardless of rest attempts
- Heightened physiological stress markers (tension, elevated heart rate during rest, digestive discomfort)
- Reduced physical stamina
- Slower recovery from illness or minor injury
- Increased susceptibility to minor ailments

**Reversibility**: Requires intentional intervention—workload reduction, prioritized rest, stress management, environmental improvement. Recovery is slower than from emerging strain.

---

### Degraded Resilience → Severe Exhaustion

**Trigger Context**: Prolonged exposure to unrelenting stressors. Chronic sleep deprivation. Multiple compounding physiological challenges.

**Transition Description**:
Physiological functioning deteriorates to a state of severe exhaustion where basic systems struggle to maintain operation. Sleep, even when obtained, provides minimal restoration. Fatigue is overwhelming and persistent. Stress response is either hyperactive (chronic arousal) or suppressed (exhaustion of stress systems). Physical capacity is significantly impaired. Cognitive performance suffers due to physiological degradation. Health risks increase.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Profound, unshakable fatigue
- Sleep is dysfunctional—either impossible to achieve or non-restorative even when obtained
- Marked decline in physical performance
- Immune function weakens (increased illness)
- Emotional regulation impaired due to physiological state
- Potential for acute health events

**Reversibility**: Requires substantial recovery period, possible medical intervention, significant workload reduction or mission modification. May not fully return to baseline during mission.

---

### Recovery Transitions

**Severe Exhaustion → Gradual Stabilization → Emerging Restoration → Stable Functioning**

**Trigger Context**: Removal of major stressors, prioritized rest, medical support, workload reduction, environmental improvements.

**Transition Description**:
Recovery proceeds in reverse through gradual phases. Initial recovery focuses on preventing further degradation and establishing minimal stability. Sleep quality begins to improve incrementally. Energy levels slowly increase. Stress response begins to normalize. As recovery continues, resilience returns, reserve capacity rebuilds, and eventually baseline physiological functioning is restored—though this may require extended time.

**Pattern**: Recovery is typically slower than degradation. Each recovery phase requires sustained favorable conditions.

## Cognitive Transitions

Cognitive state transitions describe changes in mental clarity, decision-making quality, situational awareness, belief coherence, and intentional processes.

### Clear Focus → Divided Priorities

**Trigger Context**: Introduction of multiple competing demands, conflicting information, or ambiguous priorities.

**Transition Description**:
Cognitive functioning transitions from a state of clear, focused attention on well-understood priorities to a state where attention is fragmented across multiple concerns. Mental resources are divided. Decision-making becomes more complex as trade-offs between competing priorities must be evaluated. Cognitive load increases. Mental effort feels greater for equivalent task difficulty.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Difficulty maintaining sustained attention on single task
- Increased mental fatigue from constant priority switching
- Decisions take longer due to complexity
- Some tasks receive inadequate attention
- Sense of being mentally scattered or pulled in multiple directions

**Reversibility**: Reversible through priority clarification, workload reduction, or elimination of competing demands.

---

### Divided Priorities → Disrupted Intention Clarity

**Trigger Context**: Persistent cognitive overload, unresolved goal conflicts, chronic information ambiguity, decision fatigue.

**Transition Description**:
The state of divided priorities deepens into disrupted intention clarity where the crew struggles to maintain coherent understanding of what they are trying to achieve and why. Goals feel conflicted or unclear. Beliefs about mission state, priorities, or appropriate actions become uncertain or contradictory. Intentions lack crispness—actions feel reactive rather than purposeful. Decision-making becomes increasingly effortful and uncertain. Situational awareness fragments.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Uncertainty about what actions to prioritize
- Conflicting beliefs about situation or appropriate response
- Decisions feel arbitrary or forced
- Loss of big-picture understanding
- Actions feel disconnected from clear purpose
- Increased errors in judgment

**Reversibility**: Requires clarifying communication, collaborative sense-making, external guidance, or simplification of demands. Recovery involves reconstructing coherent mental models.

---

### Disrupted Intention Clarity → Cognitive Fragmentation

**Trigger Context**: Severe information overload, prolonged ambiguity, traumatic or overwhelming events, extreme fatigue.

**Transition Description**:
Cognitive functioning deteriorates to a state of fragmentation where mental processes lack integration. Situational awareness is severely impaired. Decision-making capacity is markedly degraded. Beliefs become incoherent or detached from reality. Intentions cannot be formed or sustained. Attention is extremely scattered. Cognitive errors become frequent. Abstract thinking and planning capacity are severely limited.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Inability to maintain coherent understanding of situation
- Decisions are impulsive, poorly reasoned, or frozen
- Marked increase in errors and oversights
- Loss of temporal orientation or sequence understanding
- Difficulty connecting actions to outcomes
- Communication becomes confused or disjointed

**Reversibility**: Requires substantial cognitive rest, removal from complex demands, external decision support, and extended recovery period.

---

### Recovery Transitions

**Cognitive Fragmentation → Gradual Reintegration → Restored Clarity → Clear Focus**

**Trigger Context**: Reduction of cognitive demands, provision of clear structure and guidance, adequate rest, resolution of ambiguity.

**Transition Description**:
Cognitive recovery proceeds through gradual reconstruction of mental coherence. Initial recovery focuses on establishing basic orientation and understanding. Decision-making capacity slowly returns. Situational awareness rebuilds piece by piece. Beliefs regain consistency. Intention clarity returns. Eventually, sustained focused attention and complex reasoning are restored.

**Pattern**: Cognitive recovery benefits from structured support, clear communication, and protected time for mental restoration.

## Emotional Transitions

Emotional state transitions describe changes in mood, affect, emotional regulation capacity, and motivational engagement.

### Stable Mood → Increased Reactivity

**Trigger Context**: Stress exposure, sleep disruption, social friction, workload pressure, or frustrating events.

**Transition Description**:
Emotional state transitions from baseline stability and balanced affect to increased emotional reactivity. Mood becomes more variable. Emotional responses to events are amplified—frustrations feel more irritating, disappointments feel heavier, anxieties feel more acute. Emotional regulation requires more conscious effort. The emotional tone of the environment feels less neutral, more charged.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Greater emotional fluctuation through the day
- Irritability or impatience emerges
- Minor frustrations provoke stronger reactions
- Anxiety about problems feels more intense
- Emotional recovery from upsets takes longer
- Reduced emotional buffer

**Reversibility**: Readily reversible with stress reduction, rest, social support, or positive experiences.

---

### Increased Reactivity → Mood Dysregulation

**Trigger Context**: Persistent stress, chronic sleep deprivation, unresolved interpersonal conflict, prolonged isolation, accumulated frustrations.

**Transition Description**:
Increased reactivity deepens into mood dysregulation where emotional control becomes significantly impaired. Mood swings are pronounced. Negative emotions dominate—irritability, anxiety, sadness, apathy, or anger are frequent. Positive emotions are muted or fleeting. Emotional regulation strategies become less effective. Outbursts or withdrawal may occur. Motivation declines. Engagement with tasks and people feels effortful or undesirable.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Frequent or intense negative emotional states
- Difficulty controlling emotional expressions (outbursts, tears, withdrawal)
- Persistent low mood or anxiety
- Apathy or anhedonia (inability to experience pleasure)
- Motivation for tasks or social interaction diminishes
- Emotional numbness or overwhelming emotional intensity

**Reversibility**: Requires active intervention—emotional support, stress management, conflict resolution, rest, possibly counseling or psychological support.

---

### Mood Dysregulation → Emotional Crisis

**Trigger Context**: Traumatic events, severe prolonged stress, complete breakdown of coping capacity, overwhelming loss or threat.

**Transition Description**:
Emotional state deteriorates to crisis level where functioning is severely impaired by emotional distress. Overwhelming anxiety, profound depression, uncontrollable anger, or emotional shutdown. The individual cannot effectively regulate emotions or function normally. Thoughts may become distorted. Behavior may become erratic or withdrawn. Risk of harm to self or others may emerge in extreme cases.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Intense, overwhelming emotional distress
- Inability to function in normal role
- Marked behavioral changes
- Expressions of hopelessness, despair, or rage
- Social withdrawal or volatile social interactions
- Impaired judgment due to emotional state

**Reversibility**: Requires immediate support, possible crisis intervention, psychological care, removal from stressor environment, and extended recovery support.

---

### Recovery Transitions

**Emotional Crisis → Stabilization → Improved Regulation → Stable Mood**

**Trigger Context**: Psychological support, resolution of triggering events, social support mobilization, rest and recovery, therapeutic intervention.

**Transition Description**:
Emotional recovery proceeds through phases of crisis stabilization, gradual regulation restoration, and eventual return to emotional equilibrium. Intense distress subsides. Regulation capacity slowly returns. Mood becomes more stable. Positive emotions re-emerge. Motivation and engagement gradually recover.

**Pattern**: Emotional recovery requires both internal processing and external support. Trust and safety are essential for emotional healing.

---

## Social Cohesion Transitions

Social state transitions describe changes in crew cohesion, communication quality, trust, and collective functioning.

### Cohesive → Strained

**Trigger Context**: Interpersonal friction, workload stress affecting patience, communication challenges, minor conflicts, prolonged proximity.

**Transition Description**:
Social dynamics transition from cohesive, warm, and cooperative to strained, where relationships feel less comfortable and communication becomes more effortful. Trust remains but feels tested. Irritations accumulate. Patience wears thin. Communication becomes more transactional, less warm. Social interactions feel slightly burdensome rather than naturally supportive.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Less spontaneous social interaction
- Communication becomes briefer, more task-focused
- Minor irritations with others' habits or behaviors
- Reduced laughter or lightness in interactions
- Slight withdrawal or increased preference for solitude
- Cooperative but without enthusiasm

**Reversibility**: Readily reversible through conflict resolution, social reconnection activities, stress reduction, or time apart followed by positive reengagement.

---

### Strained → Polarized

**Trigger Context**: Unresolved conflicts, blame attribution, formation of competing perspectives, leadership challenges, inequitable burden distribution.

**Transition Description**:
Strained relationships deteriorate into polarization where the crew divides into subgroups or factions. Disagreements harden into entrenched positions. Communication between opposing sides becomes defensive, hostile, or minimal. Trust erodes significantly. Collaboration suffers as coordination breaks down. The social environment feels fractured rather than unified.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Formation of clear subgroups or alliances
- Communication across divides is hostile or avoided
- Blame and criticism are frequent
- Trust is low or absent
- Conflicts escalate rather than resolve
- Collective decision-making becomes contentious or impossible
- Sense of shared purpose weakens

**Reversibility**: Requires intentional reconciliation efforts, mediation, leadership intervention, or external facilitation. Recovery is difficult and requires rebuilding trust.

---

### Polarized → Social Fragmentation

**Trigger Context**: Complete breakdown of communication, severe unresolved conflict, betrayal of trust, prolonged polarization without intervention.

**Transition Description**:
Social cohesion collapses into fragmentation where crew members operate as isolated individuals rather than a functional team. Communication is minimal and ineffective. Trust is absent. Cooperation occurs only when absolutely necessary. The social environment is hostile, cold, or characterized by complete avoidance. Mission effectiveness is severely compromised.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Minimal communication
- Open hostility or complete avoidance
- No trust or mutual support
- Individuals prioritize self-protection over collective good
- Collaboration fails even for critical tasks
- Social isolation within confined space
- Profound sense of alienation

**Reversibility**: Extremely difficult. May require mission modification, crew changes, or intensive external intervention.

---

### Recovery Transitions

**Polarized → Tentative Reconnection → Rebuilding Trust → Cohesive**

**Trigger Context**: Successful conflict resolution, mediation, shared crisis that requires cooperation, leadership intervention, explicit reconciliation efforts.

**Transition Description**:
Social recovery proceeds through cautious reengagement, gradual trust rebuilding, restoration of communication quality, and eventual reestablishment of cohesion. Initial steps are tentative and fragile. Trust returns slowly through consistent positive interactions. Communication becomes more open. Cooperative patterns reemerge. Eventually warmth and genuine solidarity return.

**Pattern**: Social recovery requires intentional effort from all parties, time, and demonstration of changed behavior. Forgiveness and commitment to moving forward are essential.

---

## Coping and Adaptive Capacity Transitions

Coping transitions describe changes in the crew's ability to manage demands, regulate stress, and maintain adaptive functioning.

### Adequate Resources → Stretched Capacity

**Trigger Context**: Increased demands, multiple stressors, reduced recovery time, depletion of specific resources (time, energy, social support).

**Transition Description**:
Coping resources transition from adequate with comfortable reserve capacity to stretched where demands approach the limits of current resources. The crew is managing but with reduced margin for additional challenges. Adaptive strategies are deployed actively. Effort feels greater. Less bandwidth is available for non-essential activities or unexpected problems.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Tasks that were routine now require conscious effort
- Less time or energy for leisure or social activities
- Reduced tolerance for additional demands
- Coping strategies are actively employed rather than functioning passively within capacity
- Sense of operating near limits

**Reversibility**: Readily reversible through workload reduction, restoration of resources (rest, social support, time), or successful problem resolution.

---

### Stretched Capacity → Depleted Reserves

**Trigger Context**: Prolonged elevated demands, compounding stressors, inadequate recovery, chronic resource deficit.

**Transition Description**:
Stretched capacity gives way to resource depletion where coping bandwidth is nearly exhausted. The crew is barely managing current demands. No reserve exists for additional challenges. Adaptive strategies are strained and beginning to fail. Errors increase. Performance declines. Maladaptive patterns may emerge (avoidance, shortcuts, conflict).

**Observable Qualities**:
- Overwhelming sense of burden
- Coping strategies become less effective
- Increased errors and oversights
- Maladaptive behaviors emerge (avoidance, aggression, shutdown)
- Profound fatigue—physical, emotional, cognitive
- Any additional demand feels intolerable

**Reversibility**: Requires significant intervention—substantial workload reduction, external support, protected recovery time, resolution of major stressors.

---

### Depleted Reserves → Adaptive Breakthrough or Maladaptive Collapse

**Trigger Context**: Crisis point where resources are exhausted and crew must either find new adaptive strategies or experience functional breakdown.

**Transition Description**:
When reserves are depleted, the system reaches a critical juncture with two possible trajectories:

**Adaptive Breakthrough**: The crew discovers new coping strategies, mobilizes latent resources, receives effective support, or experiences stressor reduction. Creative problem-solving emerges. Social support is activated. Psychological resilience manifests. New equilibrium is established at functional level.

**Maladaptive Collapse**: The crew cannot find effective adaptation and experiences breakdown. Performance deteriorates markedly. Social cohesion fractures. Individuals withdraw or conflict escalates. Errors become dangerous. Mission objectives are compromised.

**Observable Qualities (Adaptive)**:
- Creative solutions emerge
- Social solidarity strengthens under shared challenge
- Renewed determination and focus
- Effective prioritization and resource allocation
- Resilience is demonstrated

**Observable Qualities (Maladaptive)**:
- Performance breakdown
- Social fragmentation
- Psychological distress
- Inability to function effectively
- Need for external rescue or mission modification

---

### Recovery Transitions

**Depleted Reserves → Gradual Restoration → Rebuilt Capacity → Adequate Resources**

**Trigger Context**: Sustained favorable conditions, protected recovery time, effective support, workload reduction, resolution of major stressors.

**Transition Description**:
Coping capacity recovers through phases of stabilization, gradual resource replenishment, and eventual restoration of adequate bandwidth. Rest accumulates. Energy returns. Emotional reserves rebuild. Social support is reestablished. Confidence grows. Eventually, comfortable reserve capacity is restored.

**Pattern**: Recovery requires sustained favorable conditions. Rushing recovery or reintroducing demands prematurely can trigger relapse into depletion.

## Environmental and Workload Perception Transitions

These transitions describe changes in how the crew perceives and experiences operational demands and environmental conditions.

### Routine → Variable

**Trigger Context**: Changes in operational tempo, schedule disruptions, new task types, environmental fluctuations.

**Transition Description**:
The mission environment transitions from predictable routine to variable conditions requiring greater attention and flexibility. Tasks change more frequently. Schedules are less fixed. The crew must adapt to shifting demands rather than operating on autopilot within established patterns.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Less predictability in daily routine
- Need for greater cognitive flexibility
- Increased planning and coordination requirements
- Reduced sense of control over time and tasks
- Greater attention required to navigate changes

**Reversibility**: Reversible through return to stable routine or successful adaptation to new patterns creating new routine.

---

### Variable → Demanding

**Trigger Context**: Workload increases, complexity rises, time pressure intensifies, resource constraints tighten.

**Transition Description**:
Variable conditions escalate to demanding where workload feels heavy, time feels scarce, and tasks feel complex or challenging. The crew is working hard and feeling the pressure. Margin for error shrinks. Rest time decreases. The pace feels unsustainable if prolonged.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Sense of being rushed or overloaded
- Tasks require significant effort
- Limited time for rest or discretionary activities
- Mistakes become more likely due to pressure
- Fatigue from sustained high effort
- Desire for relief or reduction

**Reversibility**: Requires workload reduction, increased support, task simplification, or time extension.

---

### Demanding → Overwhelming

**Trigger Context**: Extreme workload, impossible time constraints, compounding crises, inadequate resources for demands.

**Transition Description**:
Demanding workload becomes overwhelming where the crew cannot effectively manage the volume, complexity, or pace of demands. Tasks are incomplete or poorly executed. The situation feels out of control. Performance suffers markedly. Stress is extreme.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Inability to complete essential tasks
- Severe time pressure with impossible choices
- Profound stress and distress
- Performance breakdown
- Sense of helplessness or desperation
- Need for external intervention or mission modification

**Reversibility**: Requires immediate and substantial reduction in demands, external support, or mission modification.

---

### Recovery Transitions

**Overwhelming → Stabilizing → Manageable → Routine**

**Trigger Context**: Workload reduction, successful problem resolution, additional resources or time, external support.

**Transition Description**:
Workload perception recovers through phases of crisis stabilization, gradual return to manageability, and eventual restoration of routine sustainable pace. Immediate pressure is reduced. Time becomes less constrained. Tasks become more manageable. Eventually, comfortable routine is reestablished.

## Belief-Desire-Intention (BDI) Transitions

These transitions describe qualitative changes in intentional processes—beliefs about the world, desires and goals, and intentions to act.

### Aligned Beliefs and Goals → Internal Conflicts

**Trigger Context**: Receipt of conflicting information, goal incompatibility, value conflicts, ambiguous priorities.

**Transition Description**:
Intentional processes transition from a state of internal coherence—where beliefs align with reality, goals are clear and compatible, intentions flow naturally from beliefs and desires—to a state of internal conflict where beliefs are contradictory, goals compete, or intentions are unclear because beliefs and desires point in different directions.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Uncertainty about what is true or what to believe
- Competing desires or goals create indecision
- Difficulty forming clear intentions
- Actions feel conflicted or ambivalent
- Increased cognitive effort to make decisions
- Sense of being pulled in multiple directions internally

**Reversibility**: Reversible through clarification of information, priority setting, value clarification, or decision-making support.

---

### Internal Conflicts → Shifting Priorities

**Trigger Context**: Persistent goal conflicts, changing mission conditions, new information altering beliefs, evolving values or desires.

**Transition Description**:
Internal conflicts lead to shifting priorities where the crew's goals, beliefs, or intentions change—sometimes adaptively in response to new understanding, sometimes maladaptively as coherence breaks down. Focus moves from one priority to another. Commitments weaken or change. Consistency of purpose is disrupted.

**Observable Qualities**:
- Frequent changes in stated priorities or goals
- Abandoned or modified plans
- Inconsistency between stated intentions and actions
- Sense of instability in purpose or direction
- Difficulty maintaining long-term commitments
- Reactive rather than purposeful behavior

**Reversibility**: Requires renewed clarity about core values, collaborative goal alignment, or resolution of conflicts through decision-making.

---

### Shifting Priorities → Renewed Alignment or Fragmentation

**Trigger Context**: The shifting priorities trajectory reaches a resolution—either the crew achieves new coherent alignment or remains in fragmented state.

**Transition Description**:

**Renewed Alignment**: Through reflection, communication, decision-making, or resolution of conflicts, the crew establishes new coherent understanding. Beliefs are clarified. Goals are realigned, perhaps modified from original but now coherent. Intentions become clear and purposeful again.

**Fragmentation**: Conflicts remain unresolved. Beliefs become increasingly incoherent or detached from reality. Goals remain contradictory. Intentions are weak or absent. Action becomes reactive, confused, or paralyzed.

**Observable Qualities (Alignment)**:
- Clear shared understanding of situation and priorities
- Coherent goal structure
- Purposeful, intentional action
- Consistency between beliefs, desires, and intentions
- Sense of clarity and direction

**Observable Qualities (Fragmentation)**:
- Persistent confusion or uncertainty
- Lack of clear direction
- Contradictory or absent goals
- Paralysis or aimless activity
- Profound sense of being lost or purposeless

---

## Integration and Use

These qualitative state transition patterns provide vocabulary for describing how crew functioning evolves within scenarios. Transitions can occur:
- **Independently** in single domains
- **In parallel** across multiple domains simultaneously
- **Sequentially** where change in one domain triggers transitions in others
- **Interactively** where transitions in different domains influence each other

Understanding these patterns enables rich, nuanced description of mission scenarios without requiring numeric modeling. The transitions reflect realistic psychological, physiological, and social dynamics observed in extreme environments while remaining purely conceptual and qualitative.
